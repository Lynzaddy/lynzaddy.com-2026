Why Process Matters

These aren't
hypothetical risks.
We've seen them.

Every item on this list is something a client came to us to fix after another shop — or a well-intentioned internal team — skipped a step. Our process exists to prevent exactly these outcomes.

✕
Scope creep from vague discovery When nobody documents what "done" means at the start, it expands to mean whatever someone wants it to mean six weeks in. Projects go over budget not because of surprises — but because the original brief was a wish list, not a specification.
✕
Rebuilds because architecture wasn't planned A beautiful site built on a brittle content model will need to be rebuilt when the business grows or the team changes. We've rebuilt more sites than we care to count — the pattern is always the same: someone skipped the architecture conversation.
✕
Sites editors can't actually use after launch Developers build for developers. Marketing teams need tools, not code. Without an explicit conversation about who edits what and how, you end up with a site that requires a developer for every update — which means nothing ever gets updated.
✕
Security issues from plugin bloat Every plugin is a door. The architecture phase is where we audit what's necessary, what's redundant, and what's a liability. A site with 40 plugins isn't feature-rich — it's a maintenance problem waiting to happen.
✕
Handoffs with no documentation Most agencies document nothing. So when something breaks, nobody can fix it without calling the original developer — who may not exist anymore. Our Sustain phase exists specifically to prevent this dependency.

Phase One
What the client is thinking
"What are we even paying for in week one?"
Discover
Our answer

The questions nobody
else thinks to ask.

Discovery isn't a kickoff call. It's an interrogation — of your goals, your constraints, your users, and your organization's actual capacity to maintain what we're about to build. Week one is the most valuable week of any engagement. Most shops rush through it because it doesn't look like progress. It is the only thing that determines whether the project succeeds.

We document everything. Who are your editors and how technically comfortable are they? What does your IT team need to approve before anything goes live? What's broken about your current site and what's worth keeping? What does the site need to do in twelve months that it can't do today? These questions sound basic. The answers are almost always surprising.

By the end of Discovery, we have a written brief that both sides sign off on — not a mood board, not a rough scope. A document that says: here is the problem, here are the constraints, here is what success looks like, and here is what is explicitly out of scope. That last part is the one that protects your budget.

What you get from this phase
✓Documented project brief with signed-off scope
✓Stakeholder interview notes + decisions log
✓Technical constraints inventory (hosting, plugins, integrations)
✓Editor personas — who updates what and how often
✓Explicit out-of-scope list
✓Revised timeline with realistic milestones
⚠
Without this: Scope expands, timelines slip, and both sides are frustrated — not because anyone was dishonest, but because nobody ever agreed on what the project actually was.

Phase Two
What the client is thinking
"Why aren't you designing yet?"
Architecture
Our answer

Because amateurs go
right for the design.

Architecture is the phase that makes everything else faster. It's where we decide: how is content structured in the CMS? Which plugins earn their place and which ones don't? What does the hosting environment need to look like before launch? How does a marketing editor update a landing page without calling IT? These are not design questions. They are load-bearing decisions.

We build a content model — a map of every content type, every field, and every relationship on the site — before a single template is designed. This is what keeps editors from feeling trapped and developers from writing workarounds six months later. It's also what keeps Elementor from becoming a pile of one-off sections nobody can maintain.

The plugin audit happens here too. We start with the smallest possible stack that does what the brief requires. Every plugin we add is a deliberate decision, not a convenience. A site with thirty plugins isn't powerful — it's fragile. Security vulnerabilities, update conflicts, and performance problems almost always trace back to a plugin that shouldn't have been installed in the first place.

What you get from this phase
✓Content model with all custom fields mapped
✓Approved plugin inventory (and a rationale for each)
✓Hosting environment spec and staging setup
✓Editor workflow diagram — who can do what, where
✓IT sign-off checklist (security, access, environment)
✓Design brief — structure agreed before Figma opens
⚠
Without this: You get a beautiful site that editors are afraid to touch, IT won't approve, and a developer will need to rebuild when the business changes direction in eighteen months.
Architecture → Build

Phase Three
What the client is thinking
"How do I know this is going well?"
Build
Our answer

You're in staging.
You've seen it. You've approved it.

By the time we build, there are no surprises — because the architecture phase already answered every structural question. Build is where the decisions become code. The content model becomes custom fields. The plugin inventory becomes a configured stack. The editor workflow becomes a CMS that marketing can actually use on a Tuesday afternoon without filing a ticket.

Everything is built in staging first. You see it, test it, and break it before your customers do. We use version control throughout — every change is tracked, every decision is reversible. Launch isn't a gamble. It's a promotion of a tested, approved build. The gap between "I think it's ready" and "I know it's ready" is staging. It's the environment most shops skip because it slows them down. It's the one we insist on because it's the only thing that makes launch feel calm.

We build to a handoff standard from day one — not as a cleanup pass at the end. That means the code is commented, the CMS fields are labeled for editors (not developers), and the documentation is started before launch, not written in a hurry afterward.

What you get from this phase
✓Staged build with version-controlled codebase
✓Cross-browser and mobile QA pass
✓Performance benchmarks (PageSpeed 85+ mobile)
✓CMS fields labeled for non-technical editors
✓Pre-launch punch list reviewed and signed off
✓Zero-downtime launch plan with rollback procedure
⚠
Without this: Launch becomes a fire drill. Something breaks in production that staging would have caught in five minutes. The client sends a screenshot at 9pm. Everyone pretends this is normal.

Phase Four
What the client is thinking
"What happens when you're done?"
Sustain
Our answer

"Done" is where
most shops disappear.

Launch is a milestone, not a finish line. WordPress core updates. Plugins release security patches. Your business adds a service, changes a team member, needs a new landing page by Thursday. None of that was in scope — and all of it is completely normal. The question isn't whether your site will need attention after launch. It's whether you'll have someone to call when it does.

Every project ends with a formal handoff — documented workflows, a plugin inventory with update notes, deployment instructions, and custom training for whoever manages the CMS day-to-day. We write this documentation as we build, not as an afterthought. The goal is a team that is genuinely self-sufficient, not dependent on us to do basic tasks.

For clients who want a continuing partner, we offer Sustain retainers — a monthly block of hours for updates, monitoring, performance work, and whatever comes up. No ticket system. No minimum notice. Just the same senior-level attention that built the site, available on an ongoing basis.

What you get from this phase
✓Full written handoff documentation package
✓CMS editor training (recorded + live walkthrough)
✓Plugin update schedule and maintenance notes
✓30-day post-launch support window (included)
✓Sustain retainer option for ongoing partnership
✓Emergency contact and escalation process
⚠
Without this: The site gets stale. Updates don't happen because nobody wants to break something they don't fully understand. Security patches go uninstalled for months. Eventually, something breaks — and the developer who built it is unreachable.
At a Glance

What the full engagement
looks like.

Timelines vary by project size. These are the typical ranges for a mid-sized WordPress build — a redesign or new build for a marketing site or small e-commerce project.

Phase One
Discover

Stakeholder interviews, technical audit, scope documentation, and brief sign-off. We ask the questions that will be asked at launch anyway — just earlier, when they're cheaper to answer.

Typical duration: 1 – 2 weeks
Phase Two
Architecture

Content model, plugin inventory, hosting environment, editor workflow design. This is where we get IT sign-off before the first pixel is designed — not after the site is built.

Typical duration: 1 – 2 weeks
Phase Three
Build

Design, development, QA, and staged launch. You see every page in staging before it goes live. Launch is a promotion, not a gamble. Rounds of revisions are built into the timeline, not invoiced as extras.

Typical duration: 4 – 8 weeks
Phase Four
Sustain

Handoff documentation, CMS training, 30-day post-launch support, and optional ongoing retainer. The site is yours — and you have everything you need to run it. Or we stay involved. Both are good options.

Ongoing: optional retainer
