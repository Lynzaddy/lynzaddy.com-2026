How We Work

Amateurs go right for design. We start with the problem.

A process built to make the build the easy part.

Four steps, every engagement. No wireframes in week one. No code until the structure is right.

Each step = a .step block: big italic number left, Heading + Text + deliverable right
01

Discover

A structured conversation about the business problem — not the website. We want to understand what success looks like before we scope a single page: who you're trying to reach, what has to happen, and what winning looks like six months out.

Deliverable: Scope document + written brief
02

Architecture

Site structure, CMS plan, plugin selection, and hosting environment — all decided before design begins. This is the work most agencies skip, and it's the reason their builds fall apart six months later. We map it first.

Deliverable: Technical architecture document
03

Build

Design and development happen in tandem on a staged environment. Regular check-ins, no surprises at launch. You review your site in context — real content, real layouts — not in flat mockups that hide the hard parts.

Deliverable: Staged site for review
04

Sustain

Post-launch support, managed hosting, and ongoing development as your team's trusted WordPress partner — not a one-time vendor who disappears after the invoice clears. We're here for the long run.

Deliverable: Monthly support retainer

What a typical engagement looks like

Most projects run six to twelve weeks from kickoff to launch.

Week 1

Discover

Kickoff call, stakeholder interviews, and a written brief you sign off on.

Week 2-3

Architect

Structure, tech decisions, and the plan — before any pixels.

Week 4-9

Build

Staged development with regular reviews in real context.

Ongoing

Sustain

Launch, then hosting and support as your partner.

Why the process matters

What goes wrong when you skip the first two steps.

  • Projects that start with design have no foundation — they collapse the moment requirements change
  • Skipping architecture means rebuilding mid-project, doubling the timeline and the cost
  • No written brief means endless revisions, scope creep, and a finished site nobody agreed to
  • Vendors who disappear post-launch leave you stranded the first time something breaks
